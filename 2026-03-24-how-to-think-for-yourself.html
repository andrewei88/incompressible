Argument · Key Points

The Career Match Problem

  • Some work demands novelty (science, investing, startups, essays). Most work only demands correctness. The distinction is sharp.
  • Independent-mindedness is largely innate. Choosing work that mismatches your disposition makes you miserable.
  • Schools test intelligence constantly but never test independent-mindedness. They sometimes suppress it.
  • Conventional people experience their conformity as independent thought. The most conventional are the most confident they're independent.
  • Nerds often succeed at independent thinking because they're unaware of conventions, making conformity impossible. You can't defer to beliefs you don't know about.

Argument · Key Points

How to Cultivate It

  • Surround yourself with independent thinkers. They self-segregate when possible. Even one or two confidants help.
  • Seek people with unusual knowledge, not just demographic diversity. Read history to triangulate past perspectives against present orthodoxy.
  • Actively ask "Is that true?" about accepted ideas. Treat it as a puzzle, not tedious criticism. Notice how ideas spread through groups like waves. "Unfashionable ideas are disproportionately likely to lead somewhere interesting."
  • Indulge curiosity. It increases rather than sates. "Questions lead to more questions."
  • Rather than "do what you love," consider "do what you're curious about." Curiosity is a better compass than passion.
