How to Think for Yourself

Argument · Key Points

The Career Match Problem

  • Some work requires ideas that are both correct and novel (science, investing, startups, essays). Most work only requires correctness. There is a "fairly sharp distinction" between the two.
  • Independent-mindedness seems more nature than nurture. Picking work that mismatches your disposition makes you unhappy: the independent-minded find middle management frustrating; the conventional-minded sail into a headwind doing original research.
  • Schools constantly test and rank intelligence but generally ignore independent-mindedness, except to suppress it.
  • Conventional-minded people genuinely feel they make up their own minds. "It's just a coincidence that their beliefs are identical to their peers'." There may be a Dunning-Kruger effect: the most conventional-minded feel confident they're independent, while the genuinely independent worry they're not independent enough.
  • One technique nerds practice unintentionally: being less aware of conventional beliefs. "It's hard to be a conformist if you don't know what you're supposed to conform to."

Three Components

ComponentWhat It IsKey Insight
Fastidiousness about truthBeing careful about degree of belief, rather than letting the unlikely become impossible and the probable become certainWithout it, you get conspiracy theorists: "subject to a much more exacting master than mere convention." Can be increased by wanting it; merely thinking about it causes it to grow.
Resistance to being told what to thinkNot mere negation but "an active delight in ideas that subvert the conventional wisdom, the more counterintuitive the better"The most innate of the three; people who have it as adults showed signs as children. There is "a definite correlation between having a sense of humor and being independent-minded."
CuriosityThe source of novel ideas. "That's what people are usually feeling before having them."All small children are curious; the conventional-minded stop once they learn conventions. The independent-minded are "gluttons of curiosity, who keep eating even after they're full."

Argument · Key Points

How to Cultivate It

  • Surround yourself with independent-minded people. They tend to self-segregate when they can. You don't need many: "It's enough to have one or two you can talk to regularly."
  • Meet people who think differently, not just demographically different people. Seek those with unusual knowledge. Read history to "get inside the heads of people who lived in the past," which is worth the effort "for the same reason it's worth travelling far to triangulate a point."
  • Cultivate skepticism: when you hear something, ask yourself "Is that true?" Treat it as a puzzle, not tedious criticism. Watch how ideas spread through groups like waves. "Unfashionable ideas are disproportionately likely to lead somewhere interesting."
  • Indulge curiosity. Unlike most appetites, "indulging it tends to increase rather than to sate it. Questions lead to more questions."
  • Your motto should be not "do what you love" but "do what you're curious about." Curiosity is more individual than the other two components, so it may be the best compass for finding novel ideas.
