Three Theories of Personal Identity

TheoryCore ClaimKey Problem
Body TheoryYour physical body makes you youOrgan transplants don't change identity. Identical twins share DNA but are different people.
Brain TheoryYour brain (consciousness + memories) makes you youSplit brain: if half your brain goes to another body, both halves feel normal, but "you" stayed in one body. Brain Theory can't explain why.
Data TheoryYour memories and personality make you you, regardless of physical substrateTeletransporter: a perfect molecular copy has all your data, but if the original survives, the copy isn't you. Data alone isn't enough.

The Thought Experiments

  • Brain swap: a mad scientist puts your brain in Bill Clinton's body. You wake feeling like yourself in his body. Your family would eventually accept you're alive, just relocated. Supports Brain Theory over Body Theory.
  • Data copy: instead of transplanting the brain, the scientist copies your data onto Clinton's brain. All your memories, personality, fears in a different physical brain. Locke's memory theory says this is still you.
  • Torture test: after the data transfer, the scientist asks which body to torture. You choose your original body (you believe your identity followed the data to Clinton's body). But in a second scenario, told your memories will be wiped and replaced before transfer, you choose Clinton's body (you believe you stay with your physical brain). The two intuitions contradict.
  • Teletransporter: a machine scans and destroys your body in Boston, rebuilds it perfectly in London. One day the destroyer breaks. You're still in Boston. A copy walks London with all your memories. "That's not me, that's a replica." Data transfer alone doesn't preserve identity.
  • Split brain: you donate half your brain to your identical twin Bob. Both wake feeling normal. You panic: "Why did I stay in my body?" Brain Theory can't explain this. Body Theory can: you're your body, period.
  • Cell replacement: scientists replace your cells 1% at a time with identical replicas. At what point do you die? Gradual replacement feels survivable. Instant replacement (teletransporter) doesn't. The difference: continuity.
  • Body scattering: a machine disassembles you atom by atom, then reassembles you perfectly. If the teletransporter copy is a replica, reassembled-you has the same problem. Destruction and reconstruction, even with original atoms, breaks continuity.

The Continuity Theory

Identity isn't specific atoms, brain halves, or psychological data alone → Urban's grandfather at 90, looking at a childhood photo: "That's me." The six-year-old and ninety-year-old share almost nothing physically or psychologically. They'd be strangers if they met. → But they're connected by unbroken continuity: the six-year-old became a seven-year-old who remembered being six. A long chain of overlapping memories and gradual change links them. → You're less a static thing and more a progression, a story. Like a wooden boat where every plank is eventually replaced: still the same boat because replacement was continuous. → This is what "soul" might mean: not supernatural, but the intangible thread connecting you across time and change.

The Takeaway

  • No satisfying final answer exists. Examining clones, merged brains, and consciousness transfer left Urban "completely off-balance."
  • Philosopher Derek Parfit: "The early Buddhist view is that much or most of the misery of human life resulted from the false view of self."
  • Understanding that "you" are less a fixed entity and more a continuous process might reduce suffering created by ego attachment.
