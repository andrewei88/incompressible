The Psychology of Money

collabfund.com · 8,146 → 1,067 words · 6 min · 35 min saved
Argument · Key Points

Core Thesis

  • Grace Groner (orphaned at 12, secretary, never married, never drove a car) left $7M to charity from 80 years of hands-off stock market compounding. Richard Fuscone (Harvard/Chicago, former vice chairman of Merrill Lynch's Latin America division, retired in 40s) declared bankruptcy from heavy borrowing and illiquid investments. Both outcomes in the same year.
  • "In what other field does someone with no education, no relevant experience, no resources, and no connections vastly outperform someone with the best education, the most relevant experiences, the best resources and the best connections?"
  • Investing is not the study of finance. It's the study of how people behave with money. Managing money isn't about what you know; it's how you behave.
  • The finance industry talks too much about what to do, and not enough about what happens in your head when you try to do it.

Key Evidence

  • Monster Beverage stock rose 211,000% from 1995-2016 but lost more than half its value on five separate occasions. Buffett made $90B by reading SEC filings 12 hours a day for 70 years, often at the expense of his family. The emotional and physical price is the true cost.
  • Hard drives: 1950-1990 gained 296 megabytes. 1990-present gained 60 million megabytes. 8+8+8+8+8+8+8+8+8 = 72. 8x8x8x8x8x8x8x8x8 = 134,217,728. In 2004 Bill Gates questioned why anyone would need a gigabyte of storage.
  • Good investing isn't about earning the highest returns (one-off hits that kill confidence). It's about earning pretty good returns you can stick with for a long time. That's when compounding runs wild.
  • Born in 1970: stock market went up 10-fold in your formative years. Born in 1950: market went nowhere. "Current investment beliefs depend on the realizations experienced in the past."
  • 401(k) is 39 years old. Roth IRA isn't old enough to drink. S&P 500 excluded financial stocks until 1976. Venture capital barely existed 25 years ago. The further back you look, the more general your takeaways should be.
  • Markowitz won the Nobel Prize for portfolio optimization formulas, then split his own money 50/50 to "minimize my future regret."
  • Cisco reached $600B in 1999. Burton Malkiel pointed out its implied growth rate meant it would exceed the entire U.S. economy in 20 years. Short-term traders drove the price; long-term investors got swept up in a different game.
  • Rihanna nearly went broke and sued her financial advisor. His response: "Was it really necessary to tell her that if you spend money on things, you will end up with the things and not the money?"
  • Room for error is not a conservative hedge. It lets you endure hardship so you stay in the game long enough for low-probability outcomes to pay off. A 30% decline may be survivable on a spreadsheet but devastating to your psyche.
  • Housing fell 30%. Leveraged homeowners wiped out in 2009 couldn't access cheap mortgage rates in 2010. Lehman Brothers couldn't invest in cheap debt in 2009. Leverage erased every opportunity to recover.
  • Gallup data: Republican economic confidence jumped -33 to +47 when the presidency changed; Democrat confidence dropped +16 to -21. GDP, jobs, and stock market growth didn't materially change.
  • 40,000 self-described contrarians at the Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting, waiting in line at 4 AM to tell each other about not following the crowd. Real contrarianism means your views are so uncomfortable they cause you to second-guess yourself.
  • After 9/11, "another catastrophic terrorist attack is inevitable and only a matter of time." The Today Show pitched parachutes for office workers to keep under their desks. Mean reversion is a merciless law of finance. A stable strategy designed to endure change is almost always superior to one that attempts to guard against whatever just happened happening again.
  • Finance is entertainment: "money is like a sporting event where you're both the fan and the player, with outcomes affecting you both emotionally and directly." The purpose of investing is to maximize returns, not minimize boredom. Boring is good.
