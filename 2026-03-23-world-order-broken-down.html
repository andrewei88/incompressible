Why International Law Fails

Governance requires laws, enforcement, courts, and consequences → these either don't exist or are weak between countries → international bodies (UN, League of Nations) lack more power than the most powerful countries → when individual countries are stronger than collectives, individual countries rule → "the international order follows the law of the jungle much more than it follows international law"

ARGUMENT-PARALLEL · Key Points

When Wars Start

  • Greatest risk of military war: both parties have roughly comparable military power AND irreconcilable existential differences. The most explosive current case: US vs. China over Taiwan.
  • The first four war types typically escalate over ~10 years before military war begins. Once shooting starts, all other dimensions are weaponized fully.
  • Wars slip into being "stupid" (costing more than they provide) because of: the prisoner's dilemma, tit-for-tat escalation, the perceived cost of backing down, and fast decisions under misunderstanding.
  • "The two things about war that one can be most confident in are 1) that it won't go as planned and 2) that it will be far worse than imagined."
ARGUMENT-PARALLEL · Key Points

Principles for Avoiding War

  • Win-win requires negotiating with consideration for what matters most to each side and trading those things. See through your adversary's eyes. Clearly identify and communicate red lines.
  • Have power, respect power, use power wisely. Power wins over agreements, rules, and laws every time. But "soft power" is often more effective than "hard power" — generosity and trust produce win-win relationships that are far more rewarding than subjugation.
  • Power is best handled like a hidden knife. Showing it usually leads others to build counter-power and escalate. But sometimes showing power prevents a fight.
  • Fight early if your relative power is declining. Fight later if it's rising. If stuck in a lose-lose relationship, get out — preferably by separation, possibly by war.
  • "In war one's ability to withstand pain is even more important than one's ability to inflict pain."
SEQUENCE · Steps

The WWII Pattern (1929-1945)

  1. 1929 crash creates global depression. Nearly all countries face internal wealth conflicts, turning to populist/autocratic/nationalistic leaders — fascist right (Germany, Japan, Italy, Spain) or communist left (USSR, China).
  2. US raises tariffs (Smoot-Hawley 1930), which further depresses global conditions. Tariffs benefit protected entities but reduce overall efficiency — a pattern that repeats.
  3. Hitler appointed chancellor 1933. German unemployment drops from 25% to zero by 1938, income up 22%, equities up 70% — financed by forcing banks to buy government bonds and Reichsbank monetization. Productive debt works when invested in things that raise productivity.
  4. Japan, an island without natural resources, sees exports fall 50%. Goes broke in 1931, abandons gold standard. Turns to military seizure of resources — invades Manchuria (1931), then China (1937).
  5. ~10 years of economic/tech/capital/geopolitical war precede shooting. Germany tests limits by annexing Austria and part of Czechoslovakia. Japan expands through Southeast Asia.
  6. US escalates economic war against Japan: freezes assets, closes Panama Canal, embargoes oil (cutting 80% of Japan's supply). Japan calculates 2 years until oil runs out — must choose between backing down or attacking. Attacks Pearl Harbor Dec 7, 1941.
  7. War ends 1945. Axis stock markets close, virtually wiped out when reopened ~5 years later. US stocks extremely strong. New world order established with US at peak power.

Protecting Wealth in Conflict

  • Dalio's investment advice: "Sell out of all debt and buy gold." Wars are financed by borrowing and printing money, which devalues both debt and currency. Credit is not trusted between non-allied nations.
  • Protecting wealth during war is inherently difficult: normal economic activity is curtailed, traditionally safe investments aren't safe, capital mobility is limited, and high taxes are imposed. Wealth redistribution to fund war takes priority over preservation.
  • Losing a war typically leads to total wipeout of wealth and power. Axis stock markets closed at war's end, reopened ~5 years later virtually worthless. Allied equities rallied continuously from 1942 onward.
