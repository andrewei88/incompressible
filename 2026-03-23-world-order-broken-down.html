ARGUMENT-PARALLEL · Key Points

The Trigger: Munich 2026

  • At the Munich Security Conference, the post-1945 world order was "pronounced dead by most leaders." The Security Report 2026 is titled "Under Destruction."
  • Merz (Germany): "The world order as it has stood for decades no longer exists." Freedom "is no longer a given." Macron (France): Europe's old security structures don't exist; Europe must prepare for war. Rubio (US): we are in a "new geopolitics era" because the "old world" is gone.
  • In Dalio's framework, we are in Stage 6 of the Big Cycle: "no rules, might is right, and there is a clash of great powers." He includes the full Chapter 6 ("The Big Cycle of External Order and Disorder") from his book.
ARGUMENT-SEQUENTIAL · Chain

Why International Law Fails

Governance requires 1) laws, 2) enforcement, 3) adjudication, 4) consequences → these either don't exist or are weak between countries → international bodies (UN, League of Nations) "have not had more wealth and power than the most powerful countries" → when individual countries are stronger than collectives, they rule → "the international order follows the law of the jungle much more than it follows international law"

When powerful countries have disputes, "they don't get their lawyers to plead their cases to judges. Instead, they threaten each other and either reach agreements or fight."

  • The first four types typically evolve over time until military war begins. Once shooting starts, "all four of the other dimensions will be weaponized to the greatest extent possible."
ARGUMENT-PARALLEL · Key Points

When Wars Start

  • Greatest risk of military war: both parties have "1) military powers that are roughly comparable and 2) irreconcilable and existential differences." Most explosive current case: US vs. China over Taiwan.
  • Both fighting and backing down are costly. Managing the prisoner's dilemma well "is extremely rare."
  • Wars slip into being "stupid" (costing more than they provide) because of: a) the prisoner's dilemma, b) tit-for-tat escalation, c) the perceived cost of backing down, and d) misunderstandings when decision making has to be fast.
  • Tariffs protect domestic businesses and jobs but reduce efficiency "because production does not occur where it can be done most efficiently." They "contribute to greater global economic weakness, as tariff wars cause the countries that impose them to lose exports." But they "benefit the entities that are protected" and "create political support for the leaders who impose them."
  • "The two things about war that one can be most confident in are 1) that it won't go as planned and 2) that it will be far worse than imagined."
ARGUMENT-PARALLEL · Key Points

Principles for Avoiding War

  • Win-win: "negotiate with consideration given to what is most important to the other party and to oneself and know how to trade them." See through your adversary's eyes. Clearly identify and communicate red lines.
  • "Have power, respect power, and use power wisely." Power wins over agreements, rules, and laws every time. But "generosity and trust are powerful forces for producing win-win relationships," which are "fabulously more rewarding than lose-lose relationships." Using "soft powers" is often preferable to "hard powers."
  • "Power is usually best handled like a hidden knife." Showing it leads others to feel threatened and build counter-power. But sometimes showing power prevents a fight.
  • "It pays to fight early if one's relative power is declining and fight later if it's rising." If in a lose-lose relationship, get out, "preferably through separation, though possibly through war."
  • Financial strength to outspend rivals is "one of the most important strengths." The US beat the Soviet Union this way. Long-term success requires sustaining both "guns" (military) and "butter" (domestic welfare). Successful countries have done this for 200-300 years. "None has been able to do it forever."
  • "In war one's ability to withstand pain is even more important than one's ability to inflict pain."
CHRONOLOGY · Timeline

The WWII Pattern (1929-1945)

1929
  • Stock market crash, global depression
  • Countries turn to "more populist, autocratic, nationalistic, and militaristic leaders"
1930
  • US raises tariffs (Smoot-Hawley), further depressing other countries
  • US top marginal income tax rate was 25% (and corporate rate 12%)
1931
  • Japan's exports fell ~50% (1929-1931); went broke, abandoned gold standard
  • Japan invades Manchuria
Jan 1933
  • Hitler appointed chancellor. Germany had "nearly 25 percent unemployment, massive bankruptcies, and extensive poverty"
  • March 1933: Roosevelt's first 100 days, massive spending programs
1933-38
  • Germany: unemployment drops from 25% to nil by 1938. Per capita income up 22%. Equities rallied ~70%
  • US: after 1935 tax bill, top rate rose to 75% (from 25% in 1930). Stock market returned over 200% from 1933 to end of 1936. Economy grew at ~9% real rate
1937
  • Japan expands into China; "killing an estimated 200,000 Chinese civilians and disarmed combatants" in Nanking
  • Fed tightened money, US economy fell back into recession
1938-39
  • Germany annexes Austria and part of Czechoslovakia
  • "Before all-out wars are declared there is about a decade of economic, technological, geopolitical, and capital wars"
  • Germany invades Poland, September 1939
1941
  • US freezes Japanese assets, embargoes oil, closes Panama Canal to Japanese ships
  • "This cut off three-fourths of Japan's trade and 80 percent of its oil"
  • Japan attacks Pearl Harbor (Dec 7)
1945
  • War ends. New world order with US at peak power
  • German and Japanese stock markets closed, "didn't reopen for around five years, and were virtually wiped out"

ARGUMENT-PARALLEL · Key Points

Protecting Wealth in Conflict

  • Dalio's investment advice: "sell out of all debt and buy gold." Wars are "financed by borrowing and printing money, which devalues debt and money." Credit is not trusted between non-allied nations.
  • Protecting wealth during war is inherently difficult: "normal economic activities are curtailed, traditionally safe investments are not safe, capital mobility is limited, and high taxes are imposed." Wealth preservation is not a priority: redistribution "to get it to where it is needed most" takes precedence.
  • "Losing wars typically leads to a total wipeout of wealth and power." German and Japanese stock markets closed at war's end, "didn't reopen for around five years, and were virtually wiped out." After the 1942 Battle of Midway, "Allied equities rallied almost continuously until the end of the war."
ARGUMENT-PARALLEL · Key Points

Conclusion: Decline Is Not Inevitable

  • "Every world power has its time in the sun" but "they all eventually decline. Some do so more gracefully than others."
  • The cycle "needn't transpire this way" if countries "stay productive, earn more than they spend, make the system work well for most of their populations, and figure out ways of creating and sustaining win-win relationships with their most significant rivals."
  • "The United States, at 245 years old, has proven itself to be one of the longest-lasting."
