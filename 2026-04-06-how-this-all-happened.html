How This All Happened

collabfund.com (Morgan Housel) · 4,940 → 1,045 words · 6 min · 19 min saved
ARGUMENT · Key Points

Intro

  • Short story of the U.S. economy since WWII: uncertain, then very good, then pretty bad, then really good, then really bad, and now here we are.
  • If you fell asleep in 1945 and woke up in 2018 you would not recognize the world around you. The amount of growth was virtually unprecedented.
ARGUMENT · Key Points

1. August 1945: War ends, fear of depression

  • 16 million Americans (11% of population) served; ~8 million overseas at end; average age 23. Within 18 months all but 1.5 million were home and out of uniform.
  • Three pressures built up: housing construction halted (<12,000 homes/month in 1943); war-specific jobs vanished suddenly; marriage rate spiked — soldiers wanted homes, families, jobs immediately.
  • 1946 Council of Economic Advisors warned Truman of 'a full-scale depression some time in the next one to four years.'
  • Exports couldn't drive growth — Europe and Japan in ruins. America buried in debt, limiting stimulus.
ARGUMENT · Key Points

2. Low interest rates and the birth of the American consumer

  • In 1942 the Fed announced it would keep short-term rates at 0.38% to finance the war. Rates didn't budge a single basis point for seven years. 3-month Treasury yields stayed below 2% until mid-1950s.
  • Consumption became an explicit economic strategy after WWII. Politicians, businessmen, labor leaders all encouraged Americans to spend.
  • GI Bill: 16 million veterans could buy homes often with no money down, no interest in the first year, fixed rates so low monthly mortgage could be lower than rent.
  • First credit card introduced in 1950. Store credit, installment, personal, payday loans took off. Interest on all debt, including credit cards, was tax deductible.
  • Household debt in the 1950s grew 1.5x faster than during the 2000s debt splurge.
ARGUMENT · Key Points

3. Pent-up demand + 1930s productivity boom

  • By necessity, the Great Depression had supercharged resourcefulness, productivity, and innovation — unnoticed until the 1950s.
  • Commercial car/truck manufacturing virtually ceased 1942–45. Then 21.4 million cars sold 1945–49; another 37 million by 1955.
  • 1.9 million homes built 1940–45. Then 7 million built 1945–50; another 8 million by 1955.
  • Pent-up demand + new manufacturing capacity created jobs for returning GIs. Mixed with consumer credit, spending exploded.
ARGUMENT · Key Points

4. Gains shared more equally than ever

  • The defining characteristic of 1950s economics: the country got rich by making the poor less poor.
  • Average wages doubled from 1940 to 1948, then doubled again by 1963.
  • Top 1%'s share of total national income, after taxes, fell from 13% to 7% by 1945.
  • Real income for the bottom 20% grew by a nearly identical amount as the top 5% from 1950 to 1980.
  • Women's labor force participation: 31% after the war → 37% by 1955 → 40% by 1965.
  • Lifestyles leveled too: rich and poor used similar cars, razors, TVs, appliances. People had peers who looked like them — important because people measure well-being against peers.
ARGUMENT · Key Points

5. Debt rose, but so did incomes

  • Household debt rose 5-fold from 1947 to 1957, but income growth made it manageable; debt started from a low base after the Depression and war.
  • Even after rising in the 1950s–70s, household debt-to-income stayed below 60% (today: just over 100%).
  • Homeownership: 46.5% in 1900, ~same for four decades, then 53% by 1945 and 62% by 1970.
  • Halberstam: new homeowners 'did not fear debt as their parents had' — they believed the future had already arrived.
ARGUMENT · Key Points

6. Things start cracking (1973)

  • 1973 recession brought unemployment to highest since the 1930s; inflation surged and stayed high; short-term rates hit 8% (up from 2.5% a decade earlier).
  • Japan booming, China opening, Middle East flexing oil — America's dominance eroded.
  • Boomers came of age with different expectations as post-war tailwinds ended.
  • Expectations always move slower than facts. People still expected post-war equality of lifestyle even as growth became uneven.
ARGUMENT · Key Points

7. Boom resumes, but different

  • 1980s–90s: GDP growth strong; 1990s real GDP growth roughly equal to 1950s (40% vs 42%). Clinton: 'We have built a new economy.'
  • Same growth, totally different pockets. Between 1993 and 2012, the top 1% saw incomes grow 86.1%, while the bottom 99% saw just 6.6% growth.
  • Stiglitz (2011): top 1% incomes rose 18% over the past decade; middle incomes fell. Men with only high-school degrees: 12% decline in the last quarter-century.
  • Nearly the opposite of post-war flattening — but Americans still held onto two post-war ideas: live a lifestyle similar to other Americans, and debt to finance it is acceptable.
ARGUMENT · Key Points

8. The Big Stretch

  • Rich Americans broke away in lifestyle — bigger homes, nicer cars, expensive schools — fueled by Madison Avenue and later the internet.
  • Post-war Togetherness morphed into Keeping Up With The Joneses. A bank manager (Peter) feels entitled to live like an investment banker (Joe) because his parents' era had tight income distribution. Peter takes out a huge mortgage, $45k credit card debt, leases two cars, kids carry student loans.
  • During flat median wages, the median new American home grew 50% larger; nearly half have four or more bedrooms, up from 18% in 1983.
  • Average car loan (inflation-adjusted) more than doubled 1975–2003, from $12,300 to $27,900.
  • Share of income going to debt and lease payments: just over 8% for highest income groups, but over 21% for those below the 50th percentile.
  • Hyman Minsky described debt crises beginning when people take on more debt than they can service — like Wile E. Coyote looking down. That's 2008.
ARGUMENT · Key Points

9. Paradigm hard to reverse

  • Post-2008 response perpetuated the trends: QE boosted asset prices (boon for owners — mostly rich); Fed backstopped corporate debt (helped owners — mostly rich); tax cuts went mostly to higher incomes.
  • Mazumder: incomes among brothers are more correlated than height or weight.
  • The economy works better for some people than others. Success isn't as meritocratic and is rewarded with higher gains than before.
  • Post-war expectations of a broad middle class without systematic inequality stuck around because they felt so good for so many for so long.
ARGUMENT · Key Points

10. Stop the ride, I want off

  • The Tea Party, Occupy Wall Street, Brexit, and the rise of Donald Trump each represents a group shouting, 'Stop the ride, I want off.'
  • Stuff isn't working within the post-war expectation that it should work roughly the same for everyone.
  • Benedict Evans: 'The more the Internet exposes people to new points of view, the angrier people get that different views exist.'
  • Author isn't pessimistic: unemployment lowest in decades, wages now growing faster for low-income workers than the rich, college costs largely stopped growing once grants factored in.
  • But expectations move slower than reality, so the era of 'This isn't working' and 'We need something radically new' may stick around — which is part of what starts events like the one this story began with. History is just one damn thing after another.
